Blubb

It began with a challenge from our professor: design a new, innovative application or technology that utilised popular topics in technology to address a social issue. Our team, a group of four university students, was assigned the task of using the Internet of Things to help or provide relief to those in need of mental wellness care. We thus came up with Blubb: a safe space where people can share their problems, worries, and anxieties and receive written support back.

The first step to creating an application—or in this case, a prototype, an MVP—is to brainstorm. When thinking of the term mental wellness, the first keywords that came to mind were comfort and validation. People feel uplifted and supported when they receive kindness from others, and after establishing this, we decided we wanted to make a social app. But how could we make a social platform that was different from Twitter, Facebook, Reddit, etc.?

The answer was simple. In order to make the application unique, we decided to combine the past and present together. Our application was going to reintroduce the more intimate aspect of long-distance communication: physicality. When a user goes to reply to another person's post, their comment will then be printed out as a physical post card that will be sent to the original poster. Without breaching either user's privacy, Blubb was to act as the middle man between users, giving them an opportunity to make deeper and more personal connections.

After determining our product, we conducted research on our target users to confirm what the assumptions we made, specifically that a) letters are more personal than online comments, b) it is comforting when strangers reach out, c) people prefer anonymity when discussing their mental health. 27 participants took part in the survey*, and these we found the following results:

  • 66.6% agreed that physical letters are more personal than online messages. Furthermore, no one expressed strong disagreement with the statement.
  • Survey population had mixed feelings about feeling comfort when strangers reach out; even though the majority did agree or strongly agreed with the statement, they consisted of only 37% of all survey participants. 29.6% felt netural while 33.3% disagreed or strongly disagreed.
  • The large majority (44.4%) felt netural about preferring to discuss mental health with strangers vs people they know. 29.6% disagreed or strongly disagreed, while 25.9% agreed or strongly agreed.

Although there is a lot of varying opinions on the three statements we posed, we decided that, when combined, the minority felt negatively about handwritten letters and anonymous discussions on their mental health. Thus, we found that we justified the value of our MVP and proceeded with sketching, wireframing, and creating high-fidelity mockups.

*The survey does contain some flaws, particularly its failure to measure participants' frequency of social media usage and how they use social media. Also, our survey failed to take into account what types of social media platforms they frequent, as these also affect their following responses.
